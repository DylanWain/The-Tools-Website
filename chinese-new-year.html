Q: How do time zones work around the world?
The Earth is divided into 24 primary time zones, each roughly 15¬∞ of longitude apart. Most zones are offset from UTC (Coordinated Universal Time) in whole hours, though some use 30 or 45-minute offsets.
UTC (Coordinated Universal Time) is the primary time standard used worldwide. GMT (Greenwich Mean Time) is a time zone. While both are often used interchangeably, UTC is more precise as it's based on atomic clocks rather than solar observation.
DST shifts clocks forward by one hour in spring and back in fall, affecting time differences between locations differently throughout the year. Not all regions observe DST, creating variable time differences between the same two cities.
Countries like India (+5:30), Iran (+3:30), and parts of Australia use half-hour offsets to better align their time with local solar noon. Nepal uses a 45-minute offset (+5:45). These reflect geographic or political considerations.
Convert all times to UTC first, perform your calculation, then convert back to local time. This avoids confusion from DST changes and unusual offsets. Our calculator handles this automatically for accurate results.
