What is Holi?

Holi is one of the most vibrant and beloved Hindu festivals, celebrated with great enthusiasm across India and around the world. Known as the "Festival of Colors" or "Festival of Love," Holi marks the arrival of spring and the triumph of good over evil.

The Legend of Holika and Prahlad

The primary legend behind Holi involves the demon king Hiranyakashipu, who demanded everyone worship him. His own son, Prahlad, remained devoted to Lord Vishnu. Angered, Hiranyakashipu ordered his sister Holika (who had a boon to be immune to fire) to sit with Prahlad in a blazing fire. Instead, Holika burned while Prahlad emerged unharmed, protected by his devotion. This is why we celebrate Holika Dahan the night before Holi.

The Love Story of Radha and Krishna

Another beautiful legend involves Lord Krishna and Radha. Young Krishna, who had dark blue skin, was jealous of fair-skinned Radha. His mother playfully suggested he color Radha's face to match his. This playful coloring became the tradition of applying colors during Holi, representing divine love and joy.

How Holi is Celebrated

Holika Dahan (Night Before): Communities gather around bonfires, singing and dancing. The fire represents burning away evil and negativity.

Rangwali Holi (Main Day): The colorful celebration begins early morning. People chase each other with colored powder (gulal) and pichkaris (water guns filled with colored water). Music, dancing, and traditional foods fill the day.

Traditional Foods:

  • Gujiya: Sweet dumplings filled with khoya and dry fruits
  • Thandai: A cooling drink made with milk, nuts, and spices (sometimes with bhang)
  • Malpua: Sweet pancakes soaked in sugar syrup
  • Dahi Bhalla: Lentil dumplings in yogurt
  • Puran Poli: Sweet flatbread with lentil filling

Holi Around India

Mathura & Vrindavan (Uttar Pradesh): The birthplace of Krishna celebrates for weeks with the famous Lathmar Holi where women playfully beat men with sticks.

Barsana (Uttar Pradesh): Home to Radha, known for elaborate week-long celebrations.

Punjab: Called "Hola Mohalla," with martial arts, military exercises, and poetry.

West Bengal: Known as "Dol Jatra" or "Basant Utsav," celebrated with cultural programs and Rabindra Sangeet.

Maharashtra: Called "Rang Panchami," celebrated five days after Holi with fish and seafood delicacies.
