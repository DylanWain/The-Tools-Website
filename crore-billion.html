Writing Numbers Correctly

Number-to-word conversion follows specific rules: Numbers one through nine are typically written as words in formal text. Numbers 10 and above are usually written as numerals. However, always write out numbers at the start of sentences: "Fifteen thousand dollars was deposited" not "15,000 dollars was deposited."

Consistency matters: In any document, choose one style and stick with it. If you write "15 items" at one point, don't switch to "twenty items" later (unless one is under 10 and one is over). Technical and scientific writing often uses numerals throughout for clarity. Financial documents may require both: "Fifteen Thousand Dollars ($15,000)."

Regional Number Systems

The Indian numbering system differs from Western: 1 lakh = 100,000 (not thousand). 1 crore = 10 million (not thousand or million). When dealing with Indian businesses, banks, or documents, convert correctly: 10 million USD = 1 crore INR (at direct conversion, actual currency rates vary). 500,000 in Western = 5 lakh in Indian numbering.

Number placement conventions also differ: Western uses commas every 3 digits (1,000,000). Indian uses varying groups: 10,00,000 (ten lakh) with comma after ten-thousands place. Some systems use spaces or periods instead of commas. Know your audience and use their preferred format, especially in international business.

Check Writing Precision

Bank check amounts require exactness: Write the numerical amount in the box, spell out the full word amount on the line. "Pay to the order of" requires exact spelling. Always write out cents as "/100" (e.g., "and 50/100") even for whole dollar amounts (write "and 00/100"). Draw a line through remaining space to prevent alterations.

Common check mistakes to avoid: Don't abbreviate words. Don't use "and" except before cents (American style). Don't leave space before or after the amount. Don't write over corrections - void and start fresh. For large amounts, have a second person verify both numerical and written amounts match before signing.
