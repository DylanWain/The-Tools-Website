What Is TDEE and Why Does It Matter?

Before we dive into this tool, try the calculator above. Once you see your result, the explanation below will make more sense.

Understanding your TDEE is the foundation of any successful diet or fitness plan. Want to lose weight? Eat below your TDEE. Want to gain muscle? Eat above it. Want to maintain? Eat at TDEE. It really is that simple‚Äîthough executing it consistently is the challenge.

The Components of TDEE

Your daily calorie burn comes from four main sources:

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) - 60-70% of TDEE

These are the calories your body burns just staying alive. Even if you lay in bed all day doing nothing, you'd burn your BMR. It powers your heart, lungs, brain, and cellular processes. Larger bodies and those with more muscle mass have higher BMRs.

NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) - 15-30% of TDEE

This is all the movement that isn't formal exercise: walking to get coffee, taking stairs, fidgeting, doing chores, standing instead of sitting. NEAT varies enormously between people‚Äîit can differ by 2,000 calories daily between someone sedentary and someone constantly moving.

EAT (Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) - 5-10% of TDEE

Formal, planned exercise: gym sessions, runs, sports. For most people, this is actually a small portion of total burn. A 30-minute workout might burn 200-400 calories‚Äîless than 15% of your TDEE. This is why you can't outrun a bad diet.

TEF (Thermic Effect of Food) - 10% of TDEE

Digesting food requires energy. Protein has the highest thermic effect (20-30% of calories consumed), followed by carbs (5-10%) and fat (0-3%). This is one reason high-protein diets can aid fat loss.

The Formulas Behind the Calculator

Mifflin-St Jeor Equation (Default)

This is the most accurate formula for most people and what our calculator uses by default:

Men: BMR = (10 √ó kg) + (6.25 √ó cm) - (5 √ó age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 √ó kg) + (6.25 √ó cm) - (5 √ó age) - 161

Katch-McArdle Formula (If Body Fat Known)

If you know your body fat percentage, this formula is more accurate because it accounts for lean mass:

BMR = 370 + (21.6 √ó Lean Body Mass in kg)

Lean Body Mass = Weight √ó (1 - Body Fat %)

How to Use Your TDEE

For Fat Loss

Create a calorie deficit by eating below TDEE. The size of your deficit determines how fast you lose:

  • 250 calorie deficit: ~0.5 lbs/week loss (slow, sustainable)
  • 500 calorie deficit: ~1 lb/week loss (recommended)
  • 750 calorie deficit: ~1.5 lbs/week loss (aggressive)
  • 1000 calorie deficit: ~2 lbs/week loss (maximum recommended)

Larger deficits risk muscle loss, metabolic adaptation, and diet burnout. Start moderate and adjust based on results.

For Muscle Gain

Eat above TDEE to provide energy for muscle growth. You can't build something from nothing:

  • Lean bulk (+200-300 cal): Slower muscle gain, minimal fat gain
  • Standard bulk (+300-500 cal): Good muscle gain, some fat gain
  • Aggressive bulk (+500+ cal): Faster muscle gain, more fat gain

Without strength training, extra calories just become fat. You must lift weights to build muscle.

For Maintenance

Eat at your TDEE to maintain weight. But remember:

  • TDEE changes with weight‚Äîrecalculate after losing/gaining 10+ lbs
  • Activity changes affect TDEE‚Äîadjust for vacations or increased training
  • You don't need to hit exact numbers‚Äîa range of ¬±200 calories is fine
Pro Tip: Track your weight for 2-3 weeks while eating at your calculated TDEE. If weight goes up, your TDEE is lower than calculated. If weight goes down, it's higher. Adjust accordingly‚Äîthe calculator is a starting point, not gospel.

TDEE vs. BMR: Understanding the Difference

People often confuse BMR and TDEE. Here's the key distinction:

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) = Calories burned at complete rest. This is what you'd burn in a coma‚Äîjust enough to keep your organs functioning. It's the floor of your metabolism, not what you should eat.

TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) = BMR + all activity. This is what you actually burn in a typical day. This is what you should base your eating on.

Common Mistake: Eating at BMR

Some people see their BMR (say, 1,500) and think that's how much they should eat. But BMR doesn't include any activity‚Äînot even standing up. Even sedentary people burn 20-30% more than BMR just living their daily lives.

Eating at BMR when your TDEE is 2,000 creates a 500-calorie deficit, which might be fine for weight loss. But eating at BMR when your TDEE is 2,800 creates a 1,300-calorie deficit‚Äîthat's crash diet territory and unsustainable.

Factors That Affect Your TDEE

Factors You Can Control

Activity Level: The biggest variable. An active person can burn 1,000+ more calories than a sedentary person of the same size. Adding a daily walk (300 cal) or gym sessions (200-400 cal each) significantly increases TDEE.

Muscle Mass: Muscle burns more calories at rest than fat. Gaining 10 lbs of muscle increases BMR by about 50-70 calories daily. While not huge, it adds up and provides other benefits.

NEAT: Non-exercise activity varies enormously. Fidgeting, pacing while on phone calls, taking stairs, parking farther away‚Äîthese small things can add up to 500+ calories daily.

Diet: The thermic effect of food means high-protein diets burn slightly more calories during digestion than high-fat or high-carb diets.

Factors You Can't Control

Age: Metabolism declines about 2-3% per decade after age 20, primarily due to muscle loss. A 50-year-old has a 6-9% lower BMR than a 30-year-old of the same size.

Gender: Men typically have higher TDEE than women due to more muscle mass and larger body size on average. This isn't unfair‚Äîit's physics.

Genetics: Some people have naturally faster or slower metabolisms. However, the range is only about ¬±200-300 calories for most people‚Äînot enough to explain large weight differences.

Hormones: Thyroid conditions can affect metabolism significantly. Hypothyroidism can reduce TDEE by 10-15%. If you're doing everything right and not seeing results, get tested.
