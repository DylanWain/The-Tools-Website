üìò Understanding Pregnancy Dating

How Due Dates Are Calculated

Your estimated due date (EDD) is calculated using Naegele's rule: add 280 days (40 weeks) to the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP). This method has been the standard in obstetrics since the 1800s and remains highly accurate when the LMP date is known.

The 280-day calculation assumes a 28-day cycle with ovulation on day 14. If your cycles are longer or shorter, your actual due date may vary slightly. First-trimester ultrasound measurements can provide a more accurate estimate.

Why Count from Last Period?

Pregnancy is dated from the first day of your last menstrual period rather than conception for practical reasons. Most women know when their period started, but the exact moment of conception is rarely known with certainty.

This means that when you're "4 weeks pregnant" by standard dating, conception actually occurred about 2 weeks ago. The first two weeks of "pregnancy" are actually before you conceived‚Äîit's simply how the medical system counts.

Gestational Age vs. Fetal Age

Gestational age is measured from the LMP and is the standard used by healthcare providers. It's what this converter shows.

Fetal age (or embryonic age) is measured conception, about 2 weeks less than gestational age. Some development charts use fetal age.

üåü The Three Trimesters

First Trimester (Weeks 1-12)

The first trimester is when all major organ systems begin to develop. Despite the baby being tiny, this is a critical period of development. Common symptoms include:

  • Morning sickness: Nausea and vomiting, often peaking around weeks 8-10
  • Fatigue: Extreme tiredness as your body works hard on development
  • Breast changes: Tenderness, swelling, and darkening areolas
  • Frequent urination: Increased blood volume and hormonal changes
  • Food aversions/cravings: Sudden changes in food preferences
  • Mood swings: Hormonal fluctuations affecting emotions

The risk of miscarriage is highest during the first trimester (about 10-20% of known pregnancies). After 12 weeks, this risk drops significantly.

Second Trimester (Weeks 13-26)

Often called the "honeymoon period" of pregnancy, many women feel their best during the second trimester. Morning sickness typically subsides, energy returns, and the baby bump becomes visible.

  • Quickening: First fetal movements felt around weeks 18-22
  • Anatomy scan: Detailed ultrasound around week 20
  • Growing belly: Maternity clothes become necessary
  • Skin changes: Linea nigra, stretch marks beginning
  • Braxton Hicks: Practice contractions may begin

Third Trimester (Weeks 27-40)

The final stretch brings rapid baby growth and preparation for birth. Discomfort increases as the baby takes up more space.

  • Significant weight gain: Baby gains most weight now
  • Shortness of breath: Baby pressing on diaphragm
  • Back pain: Shifting center of gravity
  • Trouble sleeping: Difficulty finding comfortable positions
  • Nesting instinct: Strong urge to prepare for baby
  • Lightening: Baby "drops" into pelvis near delivery

üè• Important Prenatal Milestones

Key Appointments and Tests

Week 8-10: First prenatal visit. Confirm pregnancy, establish due date, order initial blood tests.

Week 10-13: First trimester screening (NT scan + blood tests) to assess chromosomal abnormality risk.

Week 15-20: Second trimester screening blood tests (quad screen).

Week 18-22: Anatomy scan (level 2 ultrasound) to check all organ systems and structures.

Week 24-28: Glucose tolerance test for gestational diabetes.

Week 35-37: Group B strep test; weekly visits begin.

Week 39-41: Non-stress tests and biophysical profiles if needed; birth planning.

Warning Signs to Report Immediately

  • Vaginal bleeding or fluid leakage
  • Severe abdominal pain or cramping
  • Severe headache or vision changes
  • Decreased fetal movement (after 28 weeks)
  • Signs of preterm labor before 37 weeks
  • Fever over 100.4¬∞F (38¬∞C)

How Due Dates Are Calculated

The standard method for calculating a due date uses Naegele's Rule, developed by German obstetrician Franz Naegele in the early 1800s. This method assumes a 28-day menstrual cycle and 280-day (40-week) pregnancy:

Formula: Due Date = First day of last menstrual period (LMP) + 280 days

Alternatively: Due Date = LMP + 1 year - 3 months + 7 days

Understanding Gestational Age vs. Fetal Age

Gestational age counts from the first day of your last menstrual period, even though conception typically occurs about two weeks later during ovulation. This means when you're "4 weeks pregnant," the embryo is actually about 2 weeks old. Fetal age (also called conception age) counts the actual date of conception.

Factors That Can Affect Your Due Date

Irregular Cycles: If your menstrual cycles are longer or shorter than 28 days, your ovulation timing differs from standard assumptions. A 35-day cycle, for example, means ovulation occurred later, potentially shifting your due date.

Cycle Length Variation: Women with cycles ranging 21 to 35 days may have due dates that differ from Naegele's Rule by up to two weeks.

IVF and Assisted Reproduction: For IVF pregnancies, the exact date of embryo transfer is known, making due date calculation more precise.

Ultrasound Dating

First-trimester ultrasounds (before 13 weeks) are the most accurate method for determining gestational age and due date. The crown-rump length (CRL) measurement can predict due date within 5-7 days accuracy. Later ultrasounds become less accurate as fetal size varies more between pregnancies.

Pregnancy Milestones by Week

Weeks 1-4: Conception and implantation occur. The embryo is microscopic.

Weeks 5-8: Heart begins beating. Major organs start forming. Morning sickness may begin.

Weeks 9-12: Embryo becomes a fetus. Risk of miscarriage decreases significantly after week 12.

Weeks 13-16: Second trimester begins. Energy often returns. Fetus can make facial expressions.

Weeks 17-20: Anatomy scan typically performed. Many women feel first fetal movements (quickening).

Weeks 21-24: Fetus develops sleep-wake cycles. Hearing develops.

Weeks 25-28: Eyes open. Lungs continue maturing. Third trimester begins at week 28.

Weeks 29-32: Rapid brain development. Fetus gains weight quickly.

Weeks 33-36: Most organs fully developed. Fetus moves into head-down position.

Weeks 37-40: Full term at 37 weeks. Baby continues gaining weight until birth.

Trimester Overview

First Trimester (Weeks 1-12): Most critical period for organ development. Highest risk of miscarriage. Common symptoms include fatigue, nausea, and breast tenderness.

Second Trimester (Weeks 13-27): Often called the "honeymoon phase." Energy returns, morning sickness typically subsides. Visible baby bump develops.

Third Trimester (Weeks 28-40): Rapid fetal growth. Increased discomfort as baby grows. Body prepares for labor.

When to Contact Your Healthcare Provider

While most pregnancies progress normally, contact your provider immediately if you experience:

  • Vaginal bleeding
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Decreased fetal movement after 28 weeks
  • Signs of preterm labor (regular contractions before 37 weeks)
  • Severe headaches or vision changes
  • Fluid leaking vagina

Full Term, Preterm, and Post-Term

Early Term: 37 weeks 0 days - 38 weeks 6 days

Full Term: 39 weeks 0 days - 40 weeks 6 days

Late Term: 41 weeks 0 days - 41 weeks 6 days

Post-Term: 42 weeks 0 days and beyond

Babies born before 37 weeks are considered preterm and may need additional medical support.

Average Duration Statistics

While 40 weeks is the standard pregnancy length, natural variation exists:

  • First-time mothers average 41 weeks 1 day
  • Subsequent pregnancies average slightly shorter
  • About 30% of babies are born after their due date
  • Only 4-5% arrive on the exact due date
  • Most babies are born within 2 weeks of the due date
